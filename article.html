The Scandinavian living room

Warm minimalism meets shoppable design. Every piece in this room is available — tap the image to buy what you see.

SN
Sofia Nilsson
Interior editor

The Scandinavian approach to living spaces has always been about one word: balance. Warmth without clutter. Minimalism without coldness. Function that feels like beauty. In this room, we've brought those principles to life with a palette of warm neutrals, natural materials, and carefully chosen statement pieces — every single one available to shop.

What makes this room work isn't any single piece — it's the conversation between them. The gentle curve of the sofa echoes the round coffee table. The brass of the lamp answers the warmth of the walnut. Even the jute rug underfoot is part of the dialogue, its texture grounding everything above it.

The anchor piece

Every great living room begins with a sofa that invites you to stay. The Oslo's gentle curve isn't just aesthetic — it creates an intimate conversation area that makes even a large room feel personal. We chose the bouclé fabric in oatmeal for its tactile warmth and visual softness.

✨ Styling tip
A curved sofa works best when it's not pushed against the wall. Pull it forward by 30cm to create breathing room and let the shape speak. Add a round side table to echo the curve.
02

The centrepiece

A coffee table anchors the seating area and sets the tone for the entire room. We chose walnut for a reason: it darkens beautifully with age, developing a rich patina that makes the piece feel more personal over time. The round shape keeps traffic flowing naturally and softens the geometry of the space.

Why walnut?
Walnut is one of the few hardwoods that darkens with age rather than yellowing. Its tight grain resists moisture, and it's strong enough for daily use without feeling heavy. Our walnut is sourced from managed forests in Oregon.
03

The light

Lighting makes or breaks a living room. Overhead lighting flattens everything; a single well-placed floor lamp creates depth, warmth, and drama. The Arc reaches over the sofa like a gentle canopy, casting a pool of warm light that makes the seating area feel like a room within a room.

The best rooms are lit like restaurants — low, warm, and from multiple sources. Never from above.

Sofia Nilsson, Interior Editor

We paired the Arc with a linen shade to diffuse the light and avoid harsh shadows. The brass arm develops a soft patina over time, shifting from bright gold to a warm honey tone that feels less "new" and more "collected."

Left: Brass arc develops a warm patina over time. Right: Natural materials layer texture throughout the room.
04

Bringing it together

A room this considered doesn't happen by accident. The colour palette stays within a tight range — oatmeal, walnut, brass, natural jute — so nothing competes for attention. Instead, each material adds a layer of texture: the nubby bouclé, the smooth wood grain, the woven rug, the warm metal. Your eye moves between them like a conversation.

The lounge chair anchors the opposite side of the coffee table, creating a natural conversation pair. Its linen upholstery is deliberately a shade darker than the sofa, adding depth without breaking the palette. The walnut frame echoes the coffee table — these are pieces that feel like they were always meant to be together.

✨ The 60-30-10 rule
60% dominant colour (oatmeal/cream on walls and sofa), 30% secondary (walnut and wood tones), 10% accent (brass and terracotta). This ratio creates visual harmony without feeling rigid or over-designed.
